Local Man Solves Own Crossword, Immediately Forgets How
By Our Puzzles Correspondent · Upper Bogsworth

Mr. Reginald Forthwright, 54, of Upper Bogsworth, completed what he described as a "fiendishly difficult" cryptic crossword on Tuesday morning before realising, some forty minutes later, that he had constructed the puzzle himself some three years prior. "The clue for seven across was particularly devious," he told reporters, adding that he had "no memory whatsoever" of writing it.

His wife, Margaret, expressed no surprise. The puzzle featured eighteen interlocking anagrams, a hidden word running obliquely through four answers, and an unexplained reference to the Schleswig-Holstein Question that neither Mr. Forthwright nor any of the assembled judges could fully account for.

Thesaurus Found Guilty of Harbouring Synonyms Without a Licence
Court Correspondent · Central Criminal Courts

In a landmark ruling, Messrs. Roget & Son were found to have knowingly concealed upward of forty-seven near-synonyms for "melancholy" within a single entry, without adequate warning to the reader. The defence argued that such clustering was standard industry practice since 1852.

The prosecution countered that no reasonable reader seeking a synonym for "sad" required a forty-seven-count indictment. The judge delivered a verdict of considerable ambivalence.

Cryptic Indicator Found Living Double Life as Ordinary Word
Language Desk

Investigations have revealed that the word "broken," long considered a reliable anagram indicator, has been simultaneously maintaining a perfectly respectable existence as an ordinary English adjective. Several observers noted that "wild," "mad," "drunk," and "out" were all living similarly secret double lives and had been doing so for centuries without complaint from the public.

Setter Hospitalised After Clue Becomes Self-Referential
Medical Correspondent · Grand Metropolitan Hospital

The celebrated setter known as "Torquemada Junior" has been admitted in a state of acute logical recursion after one of his clues began, without warning, referring to itself. The clue, for a nine-letter word, read: "Indication that the answer is, in fact, this very clue, rearranged (9)."

Doctors described the condition as rare but not unknown among setters of great ambition. "The prognosis depends on whether the patient can accept that some clues simply do not resolve," said Dr. Fenwick.

Nation Divided Over Whether "Eel" Is Fair Game for a Three-Letter Answer
Political Editor · Westminster

Parliament descended into disorder on Wednesday following the publication of a crossword using "eel" as the answer to a three-letter clue defined only as "slippery customer." The opposition called the clue "an affront to the dignity of the House."

A compromise motion was defeated after it emerged the motion's sponsor had himself used "ide" in a charity puzzle the previous Christmas. The Prime Minister declined to comment, citing the sub judice rule in a manner legal observers described as technically incorrect.

Setter's Grid Declared UNESCO World Heritage Site
Arts & Culture · Geneva

A fifteen-by-fifteen grid compiled over eleven years by retired librarian Dorothea Blythe of Nether Pendleton has been declared a World Heritage Site by an international committee of cruciverbalists. The grid features 214 interlocking answers themed around the complete works of Anthony Trollope.

Ms. Blythe said she was "rather pleased" but noted that the clue for fourteen down had never quite satisfied her and she planned to revise it at the earliest opportunity. UNESCO confirmed this would not affect the site's protected status.

Man Completes Prize Cryptic in Record Time; Wife Remains Unconvinced
Home Affairs · Sutton Coldfield

Gerald Pickwick, 47, completed Tuesday's prize cryptic in four minutes and twelve seconds, prompting immediate suspicion from his wife Hilda, who noted he had previously required three days and two library visits to complete a standard wordsearch.

An independent inquiry found no direct evidence of impropriety but noted that several entries had been written in a different pen and that ANTIDISESTABLISHMENTARIANISM had been misspelled in a manner consistent with extreme haste.

Annual Cryptic Convention Ends in Moderate Confusion and High Spirits
Correspondent at Large · Bournemouth

The forty-third annual gathering of the Guild of Cryptic Setters concluded Sunday in a spirit delegates described as "productive ambiguity." Highlights included a heated panel on using "drunk" as both an anagram indicator and a definition simultaneously.

The competitive solving session was won by a solver who then disputed four answers on grounds they were technically proper nouns. The award for Most Ambitious Clue went to a seventeen-word entry for the answer "E," which the setter declined to explain.

Crossword Editor Vows Never to Use "Iamb" for Third Year Running
Publishing News

The crossword editor of a leading Sunday supplement issued a public apology for the third consecutive year's inclusion of the word "iamb" in the prize cryptic, describing the repetition as "entirely unintentional and somewhat difficult to explain to the board."

A full audit has been commissioned. Early findings suggest that "etui," "aloe," "naevi," and "ori" are appearing with a frequency that cannot be accounted for by chance. The editor has pledged to reduce the publication's dependence on four-letter words with unusual vowel distributions, a commitment crossword editors have been making since approximately 1922.
